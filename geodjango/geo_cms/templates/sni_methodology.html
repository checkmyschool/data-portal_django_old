The School Neediness Index began as an idea for attempting to identify how vulnerable schools were throughout the Philippines. CheckMySchool wanted to know where they should focus their resources and establish stronger volunteer networks.

So, CheckMySchool traveled to 5 schools in Guimaras and Rizel to talk to school personnel about the conditions in their classrooms.

Rizal Performing Arts School &
the Student-Classroom and Student-Teacher Ratios

We started with ~Rizal Arts school~ where we met with Principal ~~. She told us about the condemned building that forced her students to overcrowd other classrooms, pushing the student-classroom ratios above the minimum threshold defined by the Department of Education.

However, ~Principal~ also told us about efforts by student's parents and community memebrs to use extra materials that would perviusly have been thrown away to create art and tools for the school.

We then went back to CheckMySchool headquarters and considered everything ~Principal~ had told us. We raised some pretty important questions:

  • How did school resources impact student's ability to learn?

  • How did factors outside of the classroom like remoteness and a student's socioeconomic status hinder their ability to come to school?

  • Can we reliably rank each school's vulnerability based on its access to resources?

To answer these questions, we flew to Guimaras to meet with more schools and coordiante with local volunteers...(names here!)...



Cabalagnan Elementary School &
the Remoteness Index and socioeconomic barriers to school access



Our first stop in Guimaras was Cabalagnan Elementary School (but not before the CheckMySchool team stopped for a famous mango pizza from Pitstop restaurant).

At Cabalagnan we met with ~Principal~ and other school personnel. Here, we were introduced to the second verainel inout School Neediness Index: remoteness. Cabalagnagn faced a challenge quite common among schools on smaler Philippine islands - many students had to cross a body of water in order to reach their school. This mean that during rainy sesason when monsoons and typhoons are common, attendance at Cabalagnan dropped drastically.

Cabalagnan teachers and community members had a solution though - an 'Adopt-a-Student' program. During bad weather, in order to prevent students from either missing school or having to cross hazardous terrain, teachers or fellow student's families would adopt a student and house them on the isalnd until the bad weather had passed.
